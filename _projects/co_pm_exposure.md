Workers and commuters in underground transit tunnels face elevated exposure to carbon monoxide (CO) and fine particulate matter (PM₂.₅) from diesel emissions, brake dust, and resuspended material. Despite known health risks, direct, high-resolution measurements in these environments remain sparse. This project deploys a network of portable low-cost sensors to characterize spatiotemporal CO and PM concentration profiles inside active tunnel environments.

Sensor units were calibrated against reference instruments in controlled chamber experiments before deployment. Measurement campaigns captured concentration gradients as a function of distance from emission sources, ventilation airflow, and time of day. Data were processed to derive personal exposure estimates for representative occupancy patterns.

Results reveal significant spatial heterogeneity in pollutant concentrations and identify ventilation inadequacies at specific tunnel segments, providing a data-driven foundation for targeted engineering controls and occupational health policy improvements.
