Polyacrylamide hydrogels are widely used in biomedical and separations applications, yet their mechanical and swelling properties are highly sensitive to synthesis conditions. This work systematically characterizes how monomer (acrylamide) and crosslinker (bis-acrylamide) concentrations affect network structure, mesh size, and equilibrium water uptake in free-radical polymerized hydrogels.

Samples prepared across a matrix of formulation conditions were analyzed using gravimetric swelling studies, rheological measurements, and FTIR spectroscopy. Swelling ratios, elastic moduli, and estimated mesh sizes were correlated against theoretical predictions from rubber elasticity and Flory-Rehner theory. Deviations from ideal network behavior at high and low crosslinker concentrations are discussed in the context of network defects and chain entanglement.

The resulting formulation-property relationships provide practical design guidelines for tailoring hydrogel mechanics to specific application requirements.
