<div class="project-sections">

<div class="project-section-card">
<h4>The Problem</h4>
<p>Methane is a potent greenhouse gas with a warming effect 120× stronger than CO₂ on a per-mass basis. Catalytic oxidation can convert it to less harmful CO₂, but current high-efficiency methods rely on synthetic zeolites costing $2,700–$3,000 per ton, produced through energy-intensive processes. This cost barrier severely limits large-scale deployment for diffuse, low-concentration methane sources like agriculture and landfills — exactly where abatement is most needed.</p>
</div>

<div class="project-section-card">
<h4>The Approach</h4>
<p>This study evaluates copper-doped natural zeolites as a sustainable, low-cost alternative ($200–$400/ton) for methane mitigation. Natural clinoptilolite samples from three geographic sources — Bear River (Idaho), St. Cloud (Arizona), and Australia — were tested to capture natural heterogeneity. Catalysts were prepared via a simple ion-exchange process with copper nitrate followed by thermal activation, using green chemistry principles throughout. Performance drivers were investigated using XRD (crystallinity), SEM (surface morphology), and BET analysis (surface area and porosity).</p>
</div>

<div class="project-section-card">
<h4>Results</h4>
<p>The Bear River (Idaho) and St. Cloud (Arizona) samples achieved <strong>complete methane conversion at 500°C</strong>, closely rivaling synthetic benchmarks at a fraction of the cost. High crystallinity and copper loading (up to 7.6 wt% in St. Cloud) were identified as key performance predictors. Notably, transition metal impurities such as iron in natural samples contributed to superior catalytic activity even without copper doping, compared to undoped synthetic counterparts. Based on these findings, a <strong>patent has been acquired</strong> for the technology, and the first-authored publication is currently under peer review.</p>
</div>
